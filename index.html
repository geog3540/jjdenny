Choropleth Mapping

In this exercise, I created a choropleth map that shows the population density of every state in the US. This map uses a color gradient ranging from light pink (low density) to dark purple (high density). States in the Northeast—such as New Jersey, Massachusetts, and Rhode Island—are the darkest, reflecting the highest population densities. In contrast, large Western states like Wyoming, Montana, and Alaska are the lightest, indicating sparse populations.

Point Symbol Mapping

In this exercise, I mapped the density of Mcdonald's restaurants locations per city in the US. This image is a proportional symbol map of the United States. It displays numerous circular symbols in black and pink scattered across the country, each symbol varying in size to represent data values at specific locations. The largest circles are concentrated in major metropolitan areas, such as along the East Coast, around the Great Lakes, Texas, and California. A small legend in the lower left corner shows the scale for interpreting the circle sizes, indicating values of 1.0, 66, and 130..

Isarithmic Mapping

In this exercise, I used an isarithmic map to show the yealy total of precipitation across Iowa. This image is a choropleth map of Iowa, divided by county boundaries, showing a gradient of values represented by different shades of color. The legend at the top indicates value ranges from approximately 37.1 to 58.8, with lighter shades representing lower values and darker shades representing higher values. The highest values are concentrated in the northeastern part of the state, while the lowest values are in the southwest and southern regions.

Flow Mapping

In this exercise, I mapped the flow of bike riders to different bike stations across the city of Chicago. This was done on Flowmapper.org. This assignment was about learning how to use location data and use a viaully apealling method to present your data.

Bivariate Symbol Mapping

In this exercise, I used a bivariate map to show the percentage of Hispanic vs. Non-hispanic residents in the state of Texas. The color scheme is explained by a diamond-shaped legend in the lower left, which indicates two variables: the proportion of Hispanic and Non-Hispanic populations. Shades of pink and purple represent varying proportions of the Hispanic population, while shades of blue and cyan represent the Non-Hispanic population. Many counties in the eastern and southeastern parts of Texas appear darker blue, indicating a higher Non-Hispanic proportion, while the southern and western counties show more pink and purple, indicating higher Hispanic representation.
